I'm excited to share that I won the **Wizard Award** for the *Most Technical Phish Story* at this year's **DEF CON 34 Phish Stories** contest! This marks my second consecutive year winning this award, following my win last year at [DEF CON 33](/blog/2025-12-10).

You can check out the full [Contest Winners Thread](https://forum.defcon.org/node/255953) and read my full [winning submission](https://forum.defcon.org/node/255875#post255882).

### The Target & Pretext
This year's scenario was centered around **The Grand Parkway 34 Multiplex**, a massive 34-screen movie theater undergoing a major cloud-streaming technology upgrade just in time for the DEF CON launch. 

My target was **Alexis Romero**, the theater's accidental, overworked IT lead. The pretext was two-fold:

1. **The Human Element (Backstory):** An "evil twin" plot where the attacker is actually Alexis's long-lost twin sister, *Alisa Romero*, seeking revenge for a childhood knitting accident that mangled her thumbs (which got her disowned by the knitting community) and, to top it off, kitten-napping Alexis's third cat.
2. **The Technical Hook (Pretext):** Masking the attack under a real event. On a Tuesday morning live-stream test, the theater experienced a brief 3.4-second freeze. The phishing email masqueraded as a BroadSwitch Studios corporate security audit warning Alexis of a critical CDN misconfiguration (using TCP instead of UDP for live video ingestion) causing the freeze, threatening credential lockout if she didn't address it.

---

### The Technical Execution
To make the campaign as technically complete as possible, I designed the attack using:

* **Authenticated Lookalike Domain:** Typo-squatted the parent company's domain (`broadswitchstudio[.]com`) and configured proper **SPF, DKIM, and DMARC** alignment. Because the email headers authenticated perfectly, it bypassed default MailScanner filtering rules.
* **Adversary-in-the-Middle (MitM) Reverse Proxy:** Instead of hosting a static clone page, the phishing link pointed to a reverse proxy proxying requests live between Alexis's browser and AWS SSO.
* **MFA Bypass:** When Alexis encountered the standard AWS MFA challenge (TOTP or push notification), the proxy passed it dynamically. Once satisfied, the proxy intercepted the resulting high-value session cookies and OAuth tokens, granting persistent cloud access even with MFA enabled.
